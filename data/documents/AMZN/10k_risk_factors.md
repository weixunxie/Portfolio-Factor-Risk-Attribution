# AMZN 10-K Risk Factors (FY2024)

## AWS Cloud Competition and Margin Risks

Amazon Web Services remains the world's largest cloud infrastructure provider by revenue and market share. However, Microsoft Azure and Google Cloud Platform are growing faster proportionally and investing aggressively to close the gap. Azure's integration with Microsoft's enterprise software stack (Office 365, Teams, Dynamics) provides a bundling advantage in the enterprise segment that Amazon does not fully replicate.

AWS gross margins are high, but are subject to pressure from price reductions (AWS has a history of voluntary price cuts as efficiency improves), increased competition, and the heavy capex requirements of AI infrastructure. Amazon has committed to spending over $100 billion on capex in 2025, predominantly for AWS AI infrastructure. The financial return profile of this investment is subject to execution risk and demand uncertainty.

Generative AI represents both a significant growth opportunity and an intensifying competitive battleground for AWS. Amazon has launched Bedrock (managed AI model service), Trainium and Inferentia chips, and SageMaker AI capabilities. Success depends on convincing enterprise customers to build AI workloads on AWS rather than on competing platforms.

## E-Commerce and Retail Risks

Amazon's retail operations face ongoing margin pressure from logistics costs, returns, and competition from Walmart, Target, Shein, Temu, and international e-commerce platforms. The company has restructured its fulfillment network multiple times in recent years to reduce per-unit delivery costs, but labor costs remain elevated and last-mile delivery is capital-intensive.

The advertising business (part of "Other" revenue) has grown rapidly and is highly profitable. This segment depends on the health of digital advertising spending, which is cyclical and correlated with broader economic conditions. Competition from Google and Meta in digital advertising is intense, and Amazon's advertising growth may slow as the market matures.

## Regulatory and Antitrust Risks

Amazon faces regulatory scrutiny on multiple fronts. The FTC filed a major antitrust lawsuit against Amazon in 2023, alleging that the company uses anti-competitive practices in its marketplace to disadvantage third-party sellers and maintain dominance in online retail. The case is ongoing and could result in significant operational changes if the FTC prevails.

The EU has opened investigations into Amazon's use of third-party seller data, the Buy Box algorithm, and Prime exclusivity practices. These investigations carry the risk of fines and mandatory changes to business practices.

Amazon's labor practices are subject to ongoing regulatory and political scrutiny. The company has faced union organizing campaigns at warehouse facilities, NLRB complaints, and legislative proposals that would increase worker protections. Labor costs represent one of Amazon's largest operating expenses, and any mandatory increases in wages or benefits would pressure margins.

## International Operations and Currency Risks

Amazon operates in over 20 countries and generates approximately 25% of revenue from international markets. The international retail segment has historically operated at a loss or thin margin, with profitability concentrated in more mature markets (UK, Germany, Japan). Currency fluctuations affect reported results and create hedging complexity.

Emerging market expansions (India, Brazil, various African markets) require heavy upfront investment with uncertain payback periods. India in particular represents a large long-term opportunity but is a highly competitive market with local players (Flipkart/Walmart, Reliance) and regulatory complexity.

## Logistics and Operations Execution Risks

Amazon's logistics network — including fulfillment centers, delivery stations, air cargo (Amazon Air), and last-mile delivery (Amazon Flex, Delivery Service Partners) — is one of the largest private logistics operations in the world. The complexity and scale of this operation creates execution risk, particularly during peak periods (Prime Day, Cyber Monday, holiday season). Any significant disruption to logistics operations — whether from labor action, weather, technical failure, or public health events — can result in customer satisfaction issues and financial losses.
