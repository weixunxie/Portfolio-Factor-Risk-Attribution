# TSLA 10-K Risk Factors (FY2024)

## Vehicle Demand and Competitive Pricing Risks

Tesla's vehicle business has experienced significant pricing pressure over the past two years. The company has made multiple rounds of price cuts globally to stimulate demand as competition in the electric vehicle market has intensified. These price reductions have meaningfully compressed gross margins in the automotive segment, which declined from approximately 30% in 2022 to the mid-teens range by 2024.

Competition in EVs has accelerated globally. In the US, legacy automakers (GM, Ford, Stellantis) have launched competing EVs across multiple segments. In China — Tesla's second-largest market — domestic competitors including BYD, Li Auto, NIO, XPENG, and others offer vehicles with comparable or superior features at lower price points. BYD has surpassed Tesla as the world's largest EV seller by volume.

Consumer demand for EVs has been slower to materialize than many bulls forecast, partly due to charging infrastructure gaps, range anxiety, higher upfront prices relative to gasoline vehicles, and interest rate sensitivity (EVs are often financed, so high rates affect affordability). A sustained period of weak EV demand growth would force further price cuts or volume declines.

## Full Self-Driving (FSD) and Regulatory Risks

Tesla's long-promised Full Self-Driving capability remains in a supervised driver-assistance mode rather than full autonomy. The company continues to invest heavily in FSD development and has extended the timeline for robotaxi deployment multiple times. Failure to achieve regulatory approval for unsupervised autonomous driving would impair a key element of Tesla's long-term value proposition.

Autonomous vehicle technology is subject to extensive regulatory oversight. Any safety incident involving FSD or Autopilot — or any regulatory investigation resulting in mandatory recalls or operational restrictions — could materially damage Tesla's brand and create significant legal liability. NHTSA has initiated multiple investigations into Tesla's advanced driver assistance systems.

## CEO Risk and Governance

Elon Musk is central to Tesla's strategic direction, public profile, and investor relations. His management of multiple other companies (SpaceX, xAI, X, The Boring Company, Neuralink) and his political activities raise questions about time allocation and focus. Musk's public statements and social media activity have at times created controversy and adversely affected Tesla's brand perception and stock price.

Governance risks include the concentration of decision-making authority in Musk, limited independent board oversight, and the precedent of his recent compensation package dispute. Institutional investor concerns about governance are ongoing.

## China Market and Geopolitical Risks

China represents approximately 20-25% of Tesla's vehicle revenue. The company manufactures vehicles at its Gigafactory Shanghai, which reduces supply chain risk but also creates regulatory exposure to Chinese government policies.

Geopolitical tensions between the US and China create the risk of regulatory retaliation, tariffs, or restrictions on Tesla's operations in China. The Chinese government has previously granted and then restricted data mapping permissions for Tesla vehicles. In a scenario of significantly escalated US-China tensions, Tesla's Chinese operations could be severely disrupted.

## Energy Business and Growth Execution

Tesla's Energy Generation and Storage segment (Megapack, Powerwall) has shown strong growth but remains a small portion of total revenue. Execution risks include manufacturing capacity ramp (the Lathrop Megapack factory), supply chain constraints (lithium iron phosphate battery cells), and grid-scale energy storage competition from established players.

The Cybertruck launch has encountered production challenges and quality issues. New model launches historically create near-term margin headwinds as manufacturing processes are optimized.
