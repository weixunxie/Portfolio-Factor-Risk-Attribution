# NVDA 10-K Risk Factors (FY2025)

## Customer Concentration and Demand Volatility

NVIDIA generates a substantial portion of its Data Center revenue from a small number of large cloud service providers (CSPs) and hyperscalers, including Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta. These customers represent a high proportion of quarterly GPU purchases. The concentration of demand in a small number of buyers creates significant revenue volatility risk: changes in purchasing patterns, inventory adjustments, or capital expenditure cycles by any single large customer can have outsized effects on NVIDIA's quarterly results.

The AI infrastructure buildout is currently in an expansion phase, but demand is inherently lumpy and subject to rapid changes in investor sentiment, macroeconomic conditions, or AI model economics. A slowdown in hyperscaler AI spending — whether from capital allocation shifts, cooling demand for AI products, or increasing model efficiency (requiring fewer chips) — could significantly reduce NVIDIA's near-term revenue.

## Export Controls and Geopolitical Risks

US government export controls are among the most significant risk factors for NVIDIA. The Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) has progressively tightened restrictions on the export of advanced semiconductors to China, requiring licenses for chips above specified performance thresholds. These restrictions have already impaired NVIDIA's ability to sell its highest-performance data center GPUs (H100, H200, Blackwell) in the Chinese market.

China historically represented approximately 20-25% of NVIDIA's Data Center revenue. The company has introduced China-compliant chips (H20, L20) that meet current export control thresholds, but these generate significantly lower revenue and margin. Further tightening of export controls — which cannot be ruled out given the evolving geopolitical environment — could reduce or eliminate NVIDIA's Chinese data center business.

NVIDIA's semiconductor supply chain is highly concentrated in Taiwan, particularly at TSMC. Taiwan Strait geopolitical tensions represent a severe tail risk. Disruption to TSMC manufacturing capacity would be catastrophic for NVIDIA's ability to supply products to customers.

## Competition and Technology Disruption Risks

NVIDIA's dominant position in AI accelerators is being challenged on multiple fronts. AMD has introduced competitive data center GPUs (MI300X, MI300A) and is gaining traction with certain workloads. Intel's Gaudi accelerators are positioned for inference workloads. More importantly, major cloud providers are developing custom AI accelerators: Google's TPUs, Amazon's Trainium and Inferentia, and Microsoft's Maia chips aim to reduce dependency on NVIDIA hardware.

The emergence of highly efficient AI models that require less compute (such as techniques like mixture-of-experts, inference optimization, and quantization) could reduce the demand for high-end training clusters. Additionally, if open-source models improve to the point where large proprietary model training becomes less economically attractive, investment in AI training infrastructure could plateau.

## Gross Margin Sustainability Risks

NVIDIA's gross margins have expanded dramatically during the AI boom, reaching approximately 75-76% in recent quarters, driven by inelastic demand for scarce H100/H200/Blackwell chips. These exceptional margins are unlikely to be sustainable as competition intensifies, supply constraints ease, and customer bargaining power increases. Margin compression during product transitions (e.g., from Hopper to Blackwell architecture) is a recurring pattern and creates near-term earnings volatility.

## Valuation and Expectations Risk

NVIDIA's stock trades at a significant premium to the broader market, reflecting high growth expectations. Any miss relative to consensus expectations — in revenue, gross margin, or forward guidance — is likely to result in significant stock price corrections given the elevated valuation multiple.
